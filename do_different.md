# What We Can Do Differently

## Purpose

ProtrackLite and the operating routines around it should create clarity,
accountability, and reliable delivery without creating unnecessary pressure,
surveillance, or administrative work.

The goal is not to return to a culture where commitments are invisible and
accountability is optional. The goal is to build **high accountability with high
autonomy**.

This document is a working agreement. It should be reviewed with employees and
changed when the process is not helping people or the business.

## What We Should Preserve

- Every meaningful commitment should have a clear owner.
- Teams should be able to see what is planned, in progress, blocked, and done.
- Delivery risks and missed commitments should be discussed honestly.
- Managers should help remove blockers and make decisions quickly.
- Monday meetings should create alignment for the coming week.
- Performance discussions should use evidence rather than memory or personal
  impressions.
- Customers and internal stakeholders should receive reliable delivery updates.

## What We Should Do Differently

### 1. Measure Outcomes, Not Visible Activity

Office attendance, hours recorded, number of tasks, and frequent status updates
are signals. They are not, by themselves, proof of good performance.

Performance should primarily consider:

- delivery against agreed commitments;
- quality and completeness of work;
- customer or business impact;
- ownership and follow-through;
- collaboration and support for others;
- early and honest communication about risks;
- learning and improvement after mistakes;
- sustainable consistency over time.

Avoid rewarding people merely for creating many tasks, booking long hours, staying
late, or appearing busy.

### 2. Collect Only Information That Is Used

Every required field or report should have a clear consumer and purpose.

Before requiring information, ask:

- Who uses this information?
- What decision does it support?
- How frequently is it actually needed?
- Is the same information already available elsewhere?
- Can the system derive or automate it?

If a field or report does not support a real decision, remove it or make it
optional.

### 3. Keep Updates Lightweight

- A routine daily update should take only a few minutes.
- Employees should not enter the same update in the app, chat, email, and a
  presentation.
- Reuse existing task and time-log data to generate reports automatically.
- Allow bulk updates and sensible defaults where possible.
- Prefer exception reporting: ask for explanations when work is blocked or at
  risk, not for repetitive narratives when work is proceeding normally.

### 4. Make Monday Meetings Useful

Monday meetings should focus on decisions and help, not on reading the application
screen line by line.

A useful agenda is:

1. What outcomes were completed last week?
2. What important commitments will be completed this week?
3. What is blocked or at risk?
4. What decision, resource, or support is needed?
5. What should be stopped, delayed, or deprioritized?

The meeting should not be used to publicly shame individuals. Performance concerns
should be discussed privately, with specific evidence and an opportunity for the
employee to explain the context.

### 5. Give Reliable People More Autonomy

Accountability does not require treating every employee identically.

- People who consistently deliver should receive greater flexibility in how and
  where they work.
- People who need support should receive clearer milestones, more frequent
  check-ins, coaching, and documented expectations.
- Additional monitoring should be based on an identified delivery problem, not
  applied permanently to everybody.
- Flexibility should be connected to role requirements and reliable outcomes, not
  managerial preference.

### 6. Separate Performance From Attendance

Some work benefits from being done together in person, but office presence should
not be used as a substitute for evaluating performance.

Define explicitly:

- which activities genuinely require in-person collaboration;
- which roles require physical presence and why;
- which days or events are expected in person;
- what flexibility exists when outcomes and collaboration remain strong.

Measure attendance only where it serves a real operational requirement.

### 7. Review Patterns, Not Isolated Weeks

- Do not draw a performance conclusion from one weak week or one missed update.
- Consider workload, dependencies, leave, production incidents, changing
  priorities, and work that was not represented correctly in the system.
- Review trends over a meaningful period.
- Distinguish between a capability problem, a clarity problem, a resource problem,
  and a motivation or accountability problem before deciding what action to take.

### 8. Reward Early Disclosure of Problems

People should not feel that hiding a delay until the last moment is safer than
reporting it early.

- Treat an early risk warning as responsible behaviour.
- Ask what changed, what has been tried, and what support is required.
- Record a revised commitment and owner.
- Hold people accountable for avoidable silence or repeated lack of ownership, not
  for every legitimate difficulty.

### 9. Use Lists and Templates as Help, Not Extra Compliance

Templates such as employee onboarding, project initiation, and employee
offboarding should save thinking time and prevent important steps from being
missed.

- Users must be able to remove, rewrite, and add items.
- A template should be a starting point, not a rigid universal procedure.
- Do not require a checklist when a simple task or conversation is sufficient.
- Periodically update templates based on the experience of the people who use
  them.

### 10. Make Managers Accountable Too

Delivery is not solely an employee responsibility. Managers should also be
evaluated on whether they:

- set clear and stable priorities;
- avoid overloading the team;
- make decisions on time;
- remove blockers;
- provide useful feedback;
- protect focus time;
- recognize good work;
- create psychological safety for honest status reporting;
- avoid changing commitments without recording the trade-off.

### 11. Protect Sustainable Performance

- Do not normalize continuous urgency or long working hours.
- Account for leave, holidays, support work, incidents, and unplanned requests
  when evaluating delivery.
- Make trade-offs explicit when new urgent work is added.
- Encourage employees to disconnect outside agreed working expectations.
- Watch for workloads that produce short-term output at the cost of quality,
  retention, or health.

### 12. Explain How Performance Information Is Used

Employees should know:

- which information is collected;
- why it is collected;
- who can see it;
- how long it is considered;
- whether it affects appraisal, compensation, promotion, or corrective action;
- how they can correct incomplete or misleading information.

Avoid hidden scoring and unexplained rankings. A person should be able to understand
and challenge the evidence used in a decision about them.

## Employee Input

Input should be collected anonymously wherever possible. Employees should also
have a named channel if they want a direct response.

Use the following questions:

1. Which part of ProtrackLite genuinely helps you plan or deliver your work?
2. Which part feels like duplication, unnecessary administration, or surveillance?
3. Approximately how much time do you spend updating the application each day and
   each week?
4. Which information do you enter in more than one place?
5. Does the Monday meeting help resolve blockers and priorities? Why or why not?
6. Do you feel safe reporting delays, mistakes, or risks early?
7. Are your weekly commitments realistic given meetings, support work, leave, and
   changing priorities?
8. Do the current measures represent your actual contribution fairly?
9. What should we stop collecting or discussing?
10. What should we automate?
11. What should managers do differently to help you deliver?
12. What is the smallest change that would preserve accountability while making
    your work easier?
13. Is there anything important that leadership may not be seeing?

Include two rating questions so improvement can be tracked over time:

- On a scale of 1–5, ProtrackLite helps me understand my priorities.
- On a scale of 1–5, our performance process feels fair and useful.

Do not collect names, email addresses, departments, or other identifying details in
the anonymous form unless they are genuinely necessary. Share a summary of the
results with everyone, including uncomfortable findings.

## How Leadership Should Respond to Feedback

After collecting input:

1. Publish the major themes without identifying individuals.
2. Separate quick fixes from changes requiring further discussion.
3. Select no more than three improvements for the first cycle.
4. Assign an owner and target date to each improvement.
5. Explain which suggestions will not be adopted and why.
6. Re-run the two rating questions after four to six weeks.
7. Show employees what changed because they provided feedback.

Feedback without visible action will reduce trust. Leadership does not need to
accept every suggestion, but it should acknowledge and answer the important ones.

## Recommended First Experiment

Run a four-week process experiment:

### Week 1: Listen

- Send the anonymous questionnaire.
- Measure the approximate time spent on updates and meetings.
- Identify duplicated data entry.

### Week 2: Simplify

- Remove or make optional at least one low-value field or update.
- Stop reading task lists line by line in Monday meetings.
- Use the decision-focused Monday agenda.

### Weeks 3–4: Observe

- Track whether commitments, blocker resolution, and delivery visibility remain
  strong.
- Ask managers and employees what improved and what became harder.
- Avoid introducing additional reporting during the experiment.

### End of Week 4: Decide

- Keep changes that reduced effort without reducing delivery clarity.
- Restore only controls whose absence caused a specific, observable problem.
- Choose the next small improvement.

## Leadership Self-Review

The CTO/CPTO should review these questions monthly:

- Am I using the application to understand and support work, or to reduce my own
  anxiety through more monitoring?
- Which decisions did the collected data help us make this month?
- Which information did nobody use?
- Are priorities stable enough for people to succeed?
- Did urgent work enter without something else being deprioritized?
- Are managers resolving blockers or merely requesting more updates?
- Are strong performers receiving trust and autonomy?
- Is any employee being judged using incomplete context?
- Can the organization operate well without my constant personal vigilance?
- What can I delegate, automate, or stop?

## Definition of a Healthy System

The system is working when:

- employees understand their priorities;
- commitments and ownership are visible;
- risks are raised early;
- managers remove blockers quickly;
- reliable delivery improves;
- high performers retain autonomy;
- reporting takes little time;
- performance discussions are fair and evidence-based;
- employees can challenge incorrect context;
- leadership can step back without delivery collapsing.

The system needs adjustment when:

- people optimize task counts or booked hours instead of outcomes;
- updates are copied across multiple tools;
- employees hide risks to avoid criticism;
- meetings become public status interrogations;
- office presence is treated as proof of contribution;
- managers add work without making trade-offs;
- employees spend significant time maintaining the measurement system;
- leadership becomes more involved in every detail rather than less.

## Commitment to the Team

We introduced structure because unclear ownership and unreliable delivery were not
sustainable. We will not abandon accountability, but we will improve how it is
implemented.

We will listen, explain how information is used, remove unnecessary work, protect
honest communication, and hold both employees and managers accountable for creating
a reliable and healthy delivery culture.
